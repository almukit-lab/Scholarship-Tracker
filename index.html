Section 05

Portfolio Strategy

The portfolio is the most important document in your application. A weak portfolio kills strong grades. A strong portfolio rescues average grades.

Most common mistake by South Asian students: Over-designed, under-conceptualised portfolios that show rendering skills but no design thinking. Admissions committees at ETH, TU Delft, Harvard, and Japanese labs are looking for evidence of architectural intelligence — not beautiful images.
A Single Clear Identity
Within 10 seconds of opening, the viewer must understand what you are interested in. This is "design authorship" — a recognisable point of view that runs through every project. If they have to search for what you are about, you lose.
Process Over Product
Show how you think, not just what you made. The sketch that became the concept. The analysis that generated the form. The failed iteration that led to the breakthrough. Process is the evidence of intelligence.
Research Depth
For academic programs, every project should have a research question behind it. Not "I designed a library" but "I researched how communal reading habits in dense Dhaka inform spatial sequencing in public buildings."
Technical Precision
Plans, sections, and construction details must be drawn correctly and beautifully. Technical drawings are the most honest signal of architectural competence. A beautiful render means nothing if your section is structurally wrong.
Complete Portfolio Structure — Page by Page
Pages 1–2
Introduction — Your Identity Statement
A single strong image + 3 sentences about who you are as an architect. Not a biography — your architectural position. What you care about, what you are researching, where you are going. Most-read part of the entire portfolio.
Pages 3–14
Project 1 — Your Strongest, Most Developed Work
Your best project gets the most space: research/concept diagram → site analysis → concept evolution → plans/sections/details → final images. Tell the complete design story. This project should demonstrate your research interest most clearly.
Pages 15–24
Project 2 — Shows Range or Deepens Theme
Either show a different scale (if Project 1 was urban, show a building; if building, show an installation) or deepen the same research theme with a different typology. Shows you are not a one-trick designer.
Pages 25–32
Projects 3–4 — Shorter Presentations (3–4 pages each)
Concept + key drawings + one final image each. These prove breadth. Cut ruthlessly — no weak work. One strong project on 4 pages is better than three mediocre projects on 12 pages.
Pages 33–36
Computational / Research / Sketch Work
Grasshopper scripts, parametric explorations, research diagrams, freehand sketches. Even 2 pages of strong hand sketches is more compelling than 8 pages of mediocre Rhino renders. Shows technical ambition and research capability.
Final page
CV Summary + Contact
One clean page. Education, key skills, publications/conferences, competitions, contact. Let the work speak — the CV is just the label on the bottle.
Storytelling — What It Means and How to Do It
Storytelling in portfolios means someone who has never seen your project understands it completely without you explaining it. Every page answers: why does this exist? The arc is: PROBLEM → RESEARCH → CONCEPT → PROPOSAL → OUTCOME → IMPLICATION. Each project is a small thesis.
Step 1: Define the Problem
What is broken in the world that your project responds to? Social (lack of community space in dense Dhaka), environmental (heat island), cultural (erosion of traditional building knowledge), or spatial. The problem gives your project moral purpose.
Step 2: Show the Research
Maps, diagrams, data, photographs, precedents. Research is evidence that you looked at the world before designing. Without it your design is just an opinion. With it your design is an argument.
Step 3: The Concept Leap
How did the research generate the concept? This is the most exciting part. Show it in one clear diagram. The best concept diagrams are memorable: viewers should be able to draw it from memory the next day.
Step 4: Proposal + Implication
Plans, sections, details answering: does this work? Can it be built? Is it spatially intelligent? Then one final sentence: what does this project mean beyond itself? This is what separates a student project from an architectural argument.

Visual Identity & Graphic System
Typography
1–2 typefaces maximum. Inter or Aktiv Grotesk for body. A serif for project titles creates hierarchy. Font size: 8–10pt body, 14–18pt titles. Consistent baseline grid. No decorative fonts — architecture speaks through space, not fonts.
Colour
3-colour palette: white background + black ink + 1 accent. Use accent sparingly — diagram highlights and key labels only. Colourful portfolios look student-level. Restrained portfolios look professional. Less is always more in graphic design.
Drawings
Line weights: 0.1mm context, 0.25mm secondary, 0.5mm primary, 0.8mm cut lines. Shadows at 30° or 45°. Do not fill every section with grey. White backgrounds unless the design demands otherwise. Every drawing must be legible at 50% size.
Diagrams
Every diagram answers ONE question. Never show everything in one diagram. Sequence: smaller to larger scale. Axonometric diagrams are most legible. Consistent graphic language across all projects — the portfolio must feel like one mind made it.
Section 06

Computational Design Roadmap

Computational skills are now a baseline expectation at most international programs. This is the strategic learning order — what to learn, when, and why.

Yes, this is critical for scholarships. At TU Delft, ETH, TUM, and all Japanese computational labs, demonstrating Rhino + Grasshopper competence is essentially a prerequisite for admission. It differentiates BUET applicants from the typical design-only portfolio more than anything else.
Month 1–3 — Start Now
Rhino + Grasshopper Fundamentals
Rhino: Model a building from a precedent (Zumthor, Piano, Siza). Learn viewport navigation, NURBS surfaces, boolean operations.
Grasshopper: Points → Curves → Surfaces → Panels. Build a parametric facade from scratch.
Key plugins: Ladybug (environmental), Weaverbird (mesh), Human UI (interface).
Resources: Mode Lab tutorials (free), parametricarchitecture.com, Rhino course on Udemy.
Month 4–6
Blender — Architectural Visualization
Blender replaces V-Ray for most students. Faster, free, more powerful for atmospheric rendering.
Learn: Cycles rendering, HDR environments, material nodes, Arch-Viz workflow, camera animation.
Workflow: Rhino → export → Blender → atmospheric render. Now standard at most international schools.
Resources: Blender Guru, ArchVizArtist, Maximilian Schreiber on YouTube.
Month 7–12
Ladybug + Honeybee — Environmental Simulation
These Grasshopper plugins allow solar analysis, wind studies, energy simulation, and daylighting analysis — extremely valuable for sustainable design research papers and for TU Delft / Germany / Japan computational labs.
Resource: Ladybug Tools documentation, Mostapha Sadeghipour Roudsari YouTube channel.
Month 12–18
Unreal Engine 5 — Real-Time Visualization
UE5 (Lumen + Nanite) is becoming the standard for high-quality architectural presentation. Epic Games Architecture licence is free.
Learn: Datasmith import from Rhino, material editor, real-time lighting, walkthrough video export. Differentiates your portfolio visually from 90% of all applicants.
Advanced — L4 onwards
Houdini + AI Workflows
Houdini: Procedural generation, urban simulation, structural form-finding. Used at ETH, AA, Zaha Hadid Architects.
AI tools: Midjourney for concept sketching, Stable Diffusion for rendering exploration. Use to accelerate ideation — not to generate design. The idea must still be yours.
Digital fabrication: If BUET has a laser cutter or 3D printer — use it constantly. Physical computational models are extraordinary in portfolios.

Section 07

Research Strategy

How architecture students start research, how to publish, and 20 research topics aligned with your Bangladeshi context and international relevance.

How to Start Research as an Undergraduate

  • 1Choose a narrow topic — not "sustainability" but "passive cooling strategies in high-rise residential buildings in Dhaka’s tropical monsoon climate"
  • 2Find 20–30 existing papers using Google Scholar, ResearchGate, or Web of Science on your topic
  • 3Write a 2,000-word literature review identifying what has been studied and what gap remains
  • 4Identify your research question: the specific gap your work will address
  • 5Choose a methodology: survey, CFD simulation, case study, comparative analysis, or historical analysis
  • 6Find a BUET faculty co-author who works in this area — do not try to publish alone as an undergraduate
  • 7Write the paper: Introduction → Literature Review → Methodology → Results → Discussion → Conclusion
  • 8Submit to a conference first (lower barrier than journals) → use that acceptance to build credibility for journal submission

Section 09

Professor Contact Strategy

The cold email to a professor is one of the most important things you can do — and most students do it completely wrong.

Most common mistake: Sending a generic email that says “I am interested in your lab and would like to join.” Professors receive dozens of these every week and delete them immediately. The emails that work are surgical — specific, brief, and demonstrate immediately that you have done real intellectual work.
Effective Cold Email — Template
// Subject: Research Inquiry — [Your specific topic] / [Name], BUET Bangladesh Dear Professor [Last Name], I am writing to enquire about the possibility of joining your [Lab Name] as a graduate student for [year]. I am currently a [year]-year architecture student at BUET (Bangladesh University of Engineering and Technology), where my research focuses on [your specific topic]. I recently read your [year] paper “[exact paper title]”, particularly your argument that [one specific idea in your own words]. My current research on [your topic] in Dhaka’s context extends this framework by [one sentence connecting your work to theirs]. I have attached a 2-page research proposal and selected portfolio work. I am applying for the [MEXT / DAAD / direct program] scholarship commencing [year] and would be honoured to discuss potential supervision. My CGPA is [X/4.0]. I recently presented at [conference] and have a paper currently under review. Thank you for your time. Sincerely, [Your name] [Email] | [Portfolio link — fast loading] | [LinkedIn]
What makes professors reply (in order):
1. You cited a specific paper of theirs — proves you read it, not just skimmed the abstract
2. You stated a clear research question — proves you can think independently
3. You attached a 2-page research proposal — proves you are serious and prepared
4. Your email is under 250 words — proves you respect their time
5. Your portfolio link loads instantly — slow links kill interest immediately
Follow-Up Template — 2 Weeks Later, No Response
// Subject: Follow-up — Research Inquiry / [Name] / BUET Dear Professor [Last Name], I wanted to briefly follow up on my email from [date] regarding potential graduate supervision in [topic area]. Since writing, I have [update: submitted to a conference / completed a new simulation study / refined my proposal based on further reading of your recent work]. I remain very interested in your research on [specific topic] and would be grateful for any response, even brief. Best regards, [Your name] | [Portfolio link]
Networking — How to Be Noticed Before the Email
Cite their work in your papers
When you publish, cite professors you plan to contact. Academics get Google Scholar alerts when their work is cited. Your name appears in their inbox before you even send the email — creating recognition before the formal inquiry.
Engage with their public work
If a professor posts on ResearchGate or gives a public talk, engage with a thoughtful specific comment. A good public comment takes 5 minutes and can determine whether your email gets opened or deleted.
Attend virtual seminars + ask questions
Many top schools host free public lectures online. Attend, ask a specific intelligent question in the Q&A, then follow up by email: “I was the student who asked about X at your seminar last week.” They will remember.
Send your published paper
Once you have a published paper, email it: “I thought this might interest you given your work on X. I am currently seeking graduate supervision and would value your feedback.” This is the most natural, least presumptuous outreach possible.

What NOT to Do — Common Mistakes

  • ✗“I am very passionate about architecture” — passion is assumed. Research alignment is what matters.
  • ✗Emailing without reading any of their actual work — professors see through this immediately
  • ✗Sending the same email to 30 professors — copy-paste emails are obvious and insulting
  • ✗Emails over 400 words — too long. Respect their time ruthlessly.
  • ✗Asking “what funding is available?” in the first email — signals you care about money, not research
  • ✗No portfolio link, or a broken/slow one — your work is the argument, make it accessible
  • ✗Following up after 3 days — wait at least 2 weeks. Professors are genuinely busy.
